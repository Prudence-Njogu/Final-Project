French Grammar Basics

French grammar may look tricky at first, but don't worry! Here are the essentials every beginner should know.

1. Definite & Indefinite Articles

In French, nouns have a gender (masculine or feminine). Articles must match the noun's gender.

Definite Articles (the)
  • le - masculine singular (ex: le livre = the book)
  • la - feminine singular (ex: la table = the table)
  • les - plural (ex: les enfants = the children)
  • l' - before vowels (ex: l'ami = the friend)
Indefinite Articles (a / an / some)
  • un - masculine singular (ex: un stylo = a pen)
  • une - feminine singular (ex: une pomme = an apple)
  • des - plural (ex: des livres = some books)

2. Gender of Nouns

Every French noun is either masculine or feminine. There is no “neuter” form.

  • Masculine: le chien (the dog)
  • Feminine: la maison (the house)

Tip: It's best to learn each noun with its article (e.g., “le livre,” not just “livre”).

3. Making Plurals

Most nouns form plurals by adding -s. Articles change too:

  • le livre → les livres (the books)
  • la table → les tables (the tables)
  • un chien → des chiens (some dogs)

4. Basic Sentence Structure

French sentences usually follow the same order as English:

Subject + Verb + Object
Example: Je mange une pomme. → I eat an apple.

5. Common Verbs

Here are two of the most important French verbs:

être (to be)
  • Je suis - I am
  • Tu es - You are
  • Il/Elle est - He/She is
  • Nous sommes - We are
  • Vous êtes - You are (formal/plural)
  • Ils/Elles sont - They are
avoir (to have)
  • J'ai - I have
  • Tu as - You have
  • Il/Elle a - He/She has
  • Nous avons - We have
  • Vous avez - You have (formal/plural)
  • Ils/Elles ont - They have

Practice Sentences

  • Je suis étudiant. - I am a student.
  • Tu as un livre. - You have a book.
  • Elle est heureuse. - She is happy.
